Apollo 11 Moon Landing

Apollo 11 was the American spaceflight that first landed humans on the Moon. Commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin landed the Apollo Lunar Module Eagle on July 20, 1969, at 20:17 UTC, and Armstrong became the first person to step onto the Moon's surface six hours and 39 minutes later, on July 21 at 02:56 UTC. Aldrin joined him 19 minutes later, and they spent about two and a quarter hours together exploring the site they had named Tranquility Base upon landing. Armstrong and Aldrin collected 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of lunar material to bring back to Earth as pilot Michael Collins flew the Command Module Columbia in lunar orbit, and were on the Moon's surface for 21 hours, 36 minutes before lifting off to rejoin Columbia. Michael Collins on 50th Anniversary Of The Moon Landing

Apollo 11 astronaut Michael Collins Narrated A Google Doodle For The 50th Anniversary Of The Apollo 11 Moon Landing, describing the Apollo 11 launch, the journey to the Moon, the tense descent to the lunar surface, and his own experience drinking coffee on the far side of the Moon.. "Behind the Moon, I was by myself -- all alone but not lonesome," said Collins. "I felt very comfortable back there; I even had hot coffee."

"The first time we saw the Moon up close, it was a magnificint spectacle. It was huge. The Sun was coming around it, cascading and making a golden halo, and it filled our entire window," said Collins. But although they were looking out the spacecraft window at a whole rocky world where no human had ever set foot, the Apollo 11 crew were more amazed by the sight of their distant homeworld. "It was nothing compared to the sight of the tiny Earth. The Earth was the main show. The Earth was it," said Collins. Forbes Article
